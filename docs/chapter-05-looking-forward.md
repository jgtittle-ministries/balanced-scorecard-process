## Where this is going: applied customer intimacy in an operational-excellence environment

The Balanced Scorecard Process grew up in the outsourcing-consulting context, where two value disciplines have to coexist that do not naturally coexist: **operational excellence** (the value discipline that contracts are written in, the basis of every SLA, the substance of what the customer is paying for) and **customer intimacy** (the value discipline that defines what kind of vendor the customer actually wants to be in a relationship with). These two disciplines are competing values in the Treacy-and-Wiersema sense — an organization that over-invests in one at the expense of the other deforms. The BSCP is the operational mechanism by which both can be held in productive tension.

The BSC is the measurement tool that lets the vendor — and lets the customer — see whether the relationship is delivering on both disciplines. Future work integrates this with a **system-dynamics model** of the outsourcing business, so the dynamic model can show the deep drivers of the customer's behavior in a quantifiable way and let both parties make better next-step decisions about a particular customer.

If we can become good at this combination, the result is a service-delivery posture that competitors operating in a single-value-discipline frame cannot match: service tuned to the detailed needs and concerns of customers, higher "wallet share" within the customer's IT spend, and a higher retention rate. The market is competitive enough that a small edge in delivery and satisfaction produces large payoffs in customer retention and share.

## Can BSCP be transported to other situations than outsourcing?

The methodology grew up in the outsourcing context, but the underlying structural insight — that what the customer actually wants is rarely articulated in the contract, and that turning those unarticulated expectations into measurable form drives the right behavior — generalizes. Any large, complex, long-term relationship can benefit, particularly in situations where matching the vendor's *behavior* (not just its deliverables) to customer expectations is critical for success.

In each context, the BSCP process does the same thing: reveals what the customer really wants, turns those expectations into a measurable form, and commits the vendor to changing behavior accordingly. The customer hears the commitment and watches the change unfold. The customer knows it is real change because the scorecard improves month over month as the vendor follows a desired behavior trajectory. At the end of a year, both parties look at the results, and the customer typically says some version of *"things are really different."* The methodology offers to revise metrics, weights, and thresholds annually so the customer knows the vendor will keep moving toward meeting their (sometimes changing) expectations. This is *applied* customer intimacy.

The natural next applications are:

- **Account-level governance for service-delivery relationships of any kind** — not just applications outsourcing.
- **Cross-organizational partnerships** where multiple business units share a common service provider but each has different operational priorities. The BSCP can roll up individual business-unit scorecards into an overall scorecard that lets senior leadership see the cross-cutting picture.
- **Customer-relationship management beyond IT services** — much as CRM itself grew up in manufacturing and then generalized.

## Where this has been used

At the time of this writing, the process has been applied to six accounts. The first three were where the methodology was evolved: a telecommunications client, an aerospace and defense engine client, and a network-equipment client. The network-equipment engagement was the first time we recognized that we had something we could sell — that the process was more than just a management reaction to specific problems.

The last three were a shift into application of a mature process, with enough history to understand structural and architectural issues. These were a county government client, a defense electronics client, and a defense contractor. Four of these six accounts were in trouble for various reasons. In each of those cases, BSCP was part of getting the engagement out of the difficulty. The methodology cannot cure all the ills of a relationship, but it has consistently helped in these cases.

More recently, the methodology has been included in new proposals — at least to the yellow-pad stage to see if the customer is interested. Feedback so far is that customers *are* interested.

## How can this be leveraged inside a consulting firm?

Three things matter:

**A champion for innovation.** The model that best describes how to leverage new methodology is Everett Rogers' *Diffusion of Innovations*. One key is to have an internal innovation champion who can sell the methodology into the business units that need it, supported by a small number of trained practitioners who can brief and provide on-the-job coaching. Without a business-unit champion, no one will call for the help, because they will not know that something exists that might lessen *this* situation's pain or bring competitive difference to *this* proposal.

**Using a delivery-assurance channel.** A corporate delivery-assurance leader has dotted-line relationships into all the business-unit delivery-assurance organizations, which together touch a very large part of the firm. The delivery-assurance role naturally functions as a "pollinator" for good ideas that solve real problems on accounts. The delivery-assurance organizations within each business unit can play the same role at their scale.

**Measuring the penetration.** There is an underlying principle that emerged as the process was developed: *metrics drive behavior.* In my experience, this is one of the few near-absolutes in the business of organizational change. Applied to the issue of leverage: the firm's research or innovation leadership could measure the adoption of innovation across the firm — the percentage penetration, proven by actual use, against targets of adopting within a one-year or two-year period. This is relatively easy to track if set up right. Once it is measurable, it can be used to incentivize business units to adopt innovations.

What I have just described is itself an essential part of the BSC Process — helping to drive a desired behavior through measurement. The basic methodology applies to a broader range of situations than the one in which it grew up.

### System-dynamics modeling application

A planned extension of this chapter describes the use of BSCP as the measurement and feedback-control element in a system-dynamics model of applications outsourcing. The model would represent the dynamic interactions among investment rate, productivity-improvement curves, organizational-change rates, contractual constraints, and customer-satisfaction dynamics — letting BSCP move from a static measurement framework into a quantitative model of the relationship's trajectory over time.

### The commitment triangle

A framing I have used in BSCP workshops is the "commitment triangle." Every manager in a service-delivery relationship makes commitments in three directions simultaneously, and is driven by all three:

1. **To employees** — interesting, challenging, somewhat stable work; competitive compensation; a good working environment; training and currency in technology; etc.
2. **To the company** — financial commitments, stockholder value, the broader "external" category including matters like equal opportunity and environmental responsibility.
3. **To the customer** — split into operational excellence (the contractual commitments), customer intimacy (the relationship discipline), and product/innovation leadership (where applicable).

Not all of these go on the BSC, but they are the recognized aspects of what drives a manager as they make day-to-day trade-offs. The BSC is the formal instrument that surfaces and balances these competing commitments at the operational level.
