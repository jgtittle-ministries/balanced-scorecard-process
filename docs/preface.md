# Preface

## A snapshot

Imagine trying to drive a car by looking at only one or two instrument readings at any time. Speed and fuel-efficiency measurements could be fine, but if you are heading west when your destination is to the east, you have failed. Too often, meeting standard Service Level Agreements (SLAs) has this same effect: all SLAs could be met, yet the client is not satisfied with the services they are receiving. How does this happen?

Often, industry-standard performance measurements don't measure all of the right things. And doing one's best isn't adequate when you don't know what you are really trying to achieve.

## Background

The Balanced Scorecard Process (BSCP) was created to manage issues that arose in application-outsourcing relationships. Such relationships are typically designed to achieve well-defined financial goals. However, there is often conflict created by a failure to specify *operational* goals.

The process works with client executives to understand their operational challenges and goals. The goals are grouped into four or five categories — for example, financial, management, quality, future value, and productivity. The client then assigns numerical weights to each category and to specific measurements within categories. Weights and measures together are identified to drive vendor behavior in the direction the client actually wants.

The data is used to build an architecture that accommodates all categories but treats each as a distinct piece. In mathematical terms, it needs to be **complete and orthogonal**, so that when one piece changes there are no automatic corresponding changes in the other metrics — each tension is held independently, as a tension, rather than collapsed into a composite.

Unlike most SLAs, these metrics are not pass-fail. We devise a graded scale and set targets and thresholds. The vendor knows that the higher the score, the happier the client.

## Demonstrating performance

The BSCP enables teams to drive performance in support of client goals. Three examples from real engagements:

- A major aerospace-and-defense client had such confidence in the impact of the BSCP that when the contract term was up, they chose not to recompete it. They renewed the contract instead, saving the money and turbulence of transition.
- With a global manufacturing company, the BSCP built the client's confidence at a time when the relationship was strained. The client was assured by the ability to use the process to target specific issues and provide visibility into the detail they wanted to see.
- Within one of the largest outsourcing accounts I have worked on, the BSCP brought together ten independently functioning business units, aligned their common goals, and unified their operations.

In summary: IT providers may be exceeding service levels but not contributing fully to the client's business goals. The Balanced Scorecard Process helps ensure continuous measurement of what is most important to clients and builds trust in the relationships.
