# Chapter 3: Case Studies

!!! warning "Draft — case study collection in development"
    This chapter presents the BSCP applied across a series of real client engagements, with each case study covering: where was the pain, what solutions had been tried before, why those did not work, what BSCP did differently, and how it worked.

    Two case studies are partially drafted below — a federal-sector environmental agency and a global steel manufacturer. ## Across the cases — what tends to be the same

These observations are drawn from the BSCP work across many engagements:

- **Set the sponsorship up front.** Set the sponsorship up front. Set the sponsorship up front. Any questions? The single largest predictor of whether the BSCP process produces a usable scorecard is whether the right people are at the table from the first meeting forward.
- **The workshops are about listening in a very proactive way.** The facilitator's first job is to keep the conversation in the territory of operational pain — not strategy abstractions, not contract terms, not vendor performance defenses. Where does it actually hurt? Whose Monday morning is being made harder by what specifically?
- **Metrics definition is interactive, and hard.** Be ready to innovate when the standard metrics do not fit. The metrics that get adopted are the ones that flow from the pain. Do not jam-fit standard metrics because they are familiar; that is where we are coming *from*, not *to*.
- **Score, weight, and re-weight as often as you need to.** The pairwise comparison process is itself part of the discovery — clarity emerges as the team works through the trade-offs.
- **The customer team always thinks they can do this faster than has ever been done before.** They cannot. The process really does require about fifteen contact days of face-to-face meetings, with homework between meetings, spread over four to six months. Believing otherwise is the single most common reason the work stalls.

## Across the cases — what tends to be different

- **Sponsorship delegation of real authority varies.** In a command-and-control organization, the delegated authority of the CIO surrogates may be much less than what the sponsor verbally claims. Test something small early to find out the authority reality.
- **Contractual constraints will impact the work of the team.** This must be understood early. If at least some of the team members do not have this understanding deeply, the process will take much longer, and may not produce a usable product.
- **The processes in place operationally have to be deeply understood by some of the team members.** These are unique to the account and situation. You cannot define real useful metrics at the level of detail needed if you do not know the real process involved. This is one reason that copying a metric from another environment does not work, regardless of how much it sounds like it would speed things up.

## Case study: a federal-sector environmental agency

### Where was the pain?

A government agency was in the early development and deployment of a network for specific agency mission-connected use. They were under federal performance-assessment guidelines and would have to show measurable approaches within several years. They evaluated the options and, on the consulting team's recommendation, took the up-front sponsorship briefing.

### What had been tried before BSCP?

This was a case where there were few measurements in place already. Not much had been tried, and so there was nothing to compare the BSC to. The work was effectively starting from a measurement-baseline gap.

### What did work — and what was hard

The initial brainstorming sessions generated about three dozen candidate metrics. However, since there was little immediate pain around the lack of measurement — the concern was mostly about the *possibility* of a future federal assessment several years out — the process moved slowly. Two-hour meetings were hard to get scheduled; all the principals were overbooked nearly all the time, and when meetings did occur, people would come in late and leave early. The in-and-out pattern and the large gaps between meetings meant most meetings required a significant review time up front to catch everyone back up to what had already been decided.

Eventually I recommended a one-day session to push through to finalization of the metrics and the weightings. The complexity was too much to keep retraining people on at two-hour intervals. We would not have finished, and even if we had, there would not have been enough shared ownership to make the BSC stick.

The one-day session got us moving and accomplished more than we could have gotten done in weeks of two-hour meetings. Follow-up meetings stayed in the two-hour pattern, but we could now chunk the work and step through the material one piece at a time.

This customer actually needed two BSCs for two related but different situations. There was some overlap in the metrics desired, but also significant difference — roughly 50% overlap. The reuse was helpful in moving us along, but it was still a long, slow process.

### How is it working?

Still in process at the time of this writing, but clarity around strategic focus is emerging.

## Case study: a global steel manufacturer

### Where was the pain?

The IT organization had inherited more than 2,000 SLAs from the parent contract. Nobody looked at them. The CIO had no way to understand what was happening in his own operation. The delivery team was being pulled back and forth on a day-to-day basis with no operationally useful prioritization signal.

### What had been tried before BSCP?

Standard IT metrics were in place, down to the micro level of the systems — all the things that an internal IT shop puts in place when it is performing its function in the conventional way. The metrics were there. The signal was not.
