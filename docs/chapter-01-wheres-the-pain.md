# Chapter 1: Where's the Pain?

## Four real situations

These are the kinds of challenges we face in this business.

**Case 1.** A fresh new contract with defined commitments and how to measure those commitments — but after contract negotiations and signature, the operational people did not accept the standard industry measurements of productivity that were in the contract. The operational people considered their work to be unique and not related to those standard metrics.

**Case 2.** We were "Green" on our contractual Service Level Agreements (SLAs), but the customer was unhappy. In this particular case the customer was ready to fire us — the contract was up for re-compete and they were not sure they would even let us bid. Think about that: our industry-standard SLAs were showing us Green across the board, and the customer was unhappy enough to fire us.

**Case 3.** A customer invoked a $1 million penalty at the end of the first contract year, based on a measure that had never been defined — only given a title. The second year of the contract had started and the accruals were already underway.

**Case 4.** A customer organization that was really ten business units assembled together prior to outsourcing, and never integrated. The CIO had only a dotted-line authority to the business-unit CIOs and zero budget authority — he could not direct anything. But the outsourcing contract was at the corporate CIO level, and that is the level at which the cost savings and benefits had been promised. So ten independent P&L units were now under a single contract and had never had to work together under a single contractual relationship. A recipe for failure if ever one existed.

All four of these are real, urgent business problems. The Balanced Scorecard Process was born from situations like these — from real people trying to make things work in the fire of real work being done. If you don't understand the background and the impact of the process, you won't be able to appreciate the power of what it brings to the relationship.

## The results

Let me give the results early, before we walk through the methodology.

**Case 1.** We proposed BSCP, ran the process, and built the first Balanced Scorecard. The initial score was an "F." My management was upset with me: "What have you done to us, John?" My answer was that that is what came out of the process — the customer looked at the first score and said, "Well, we really do believe this is a good instrument, because that is what we think of your performance right now." This was about four months into the contract's first year and things were very contentious. Twelve months later the account scored its first "A," threw a party, and the customer was glad to come. They had seen the change and were glad to acknowledge it.

**Case 2.** We were not touching the things they really cared about. At the end of the engagement they said, "We now believe you will deliver." We believe you will measure the things we care about and change the things that need to be changed — and they decided to go sole-source instead of competing the follow-on contract. For us, that was a very big deal.

**Case 3** — the case with the undefined productivity measure and the 5% penalty. This was a "titled" measure in the original contract, and because we did not meet it, the customer invoked over $1 million in penalties at the end of the first year. We proposed to implement a Balanced Scorecard to deal with the basic problem of not being able to agree on a productivity-measure definition. The pressure was on. The clock was ticking. Three months into the BSC process — before we had even finished defining the new metrics — the customer said: "We remove the penalty." They were convinced this would do what they wanted. They believed the process would measure what they cared about, and that we would now do what they wanted us to do. In the end, the process helped them define what they really wanted to do — internal clarity they had not been able to reach on their own.

**Case 4** started with the workshop I call "Where's the Pain?" It can be an all-day session of capturing the issues and pain from the customer, and in this case it was — all day. The workshop is basically a probing for what's up, what's going on, what's happening. We cover the wall with flip charts in a basic brainstorming mode. You may have been in some of those all-day sessions. At the end of this one, the delivery team went out for dinner together, and one of the team members said: *"Yeah, that felt like an all-day marriage therapy session."* That was really hard.

But at the end of it, those ten business units — units that had never had to come to agreement in this area — did. Ten separate P&L centers, with at most dotted-line authority to the CIO who owned the contract, came into alignment and built a single Balanced Scorecard. We had offered, and the process allows for, individual scorecards that roll up into an overall one. They did not need that. Their CIO was so thrilled, he wanted us to submit the process internally in their company for an excellence award.

That's the problem and the result. Everything else we talk about is just the details.

## The challenge, in one quote

Here is the challenge as a senior leader I worked with framed it:

> "With any client relationship it's important to communicate clearly and make sure that expectations for performance are well aligned. We have learned that this requires a holistic and balanced approach to measurement. This is now part of a formal governance mechanism that lives and breathes with the reality of changes in the client and business situations."

Holistic measurement. Offered as part of the way the relationship is governed. Not every customer wants this — and that's OK; standard industry service levels work fine in a lot of cases, particularly on smaller and less complex accounts. But for the engagements where the relationship is more like a marriage than a contract, where competing pressures are present and real money is on the table, BSCP is the methodology that has consistently produced the results.

## What this process adds

What we add to standard scorecard practice is a serious focus on **transforming the organizational behavior**. We keep this behavior focus throughout the process, so the result is directly tied to what will actually change behavior in the organization.

Let me rephrase the business challenge:

- Customers seldom hand to us a standard top-down strategy-driven Balanced Scorecard per Kaplan and Norton.
- If they do, it may not link to the part of the organization we directly support — at least not in an operational or transformational way.
- Net: we have to create the **strategic linkage** to the customer that helps us help them do what they want to do — in an operationally useful way, that communicates down to the line manager what to do and how to direct their management attention day to day, that works within the parameters of an already-defined contract, and that balances what we can commit to and deliver with what the customer has on their plate.

Most BSCs I have seen do not touch enough of the day-to-day lives of the line managers and task leads who make first-line things happen in the organization. We stay focused on things that actually touch the lives of those first-line managers — that is one of the keys to making the organizational transformation happen. When we do that, we also have to balance the other pressures on their work: the other things they have to satisfy in order to still deliver what we have promised in the contract. The contract is generally considered a relatively fixed constraint. It can be renegotiated when there is a compelling reason, but we try to stay within what has already been negotiated.

I often talk about there being a certain amount of *management bandwidth* taken up just running the organization — the day-to-day business of hiring, paying, directing, and the rest. What the Balanced Scorecard does is allocate the **discretionary** bandwidth above that fixed-cost bandwidth. This focus on the management team's actual decision space is fundamental to how we structure the metrics, targets, and thresholds. I sometimes say: *"Let's not put this on the BSC unless we really mean it"* — because the team's bandwidth will in fact be absorbed by the status and results of whatever the BSC names.

## A measurement-theoretic foundation

I'm an engineer, and my metrics background is fairly rich. I put a measurement-theoretic base under this process so that we have a consistent way to do apples-to-oranges trade-offs, and so that ratio scales can roll up into higher scores in a useful way. It is one of the constructs that allows a subordinate business-unit BSC to roll into a higher-level one with the scores intact. It also supports the reverse: drilling down all the way from the highest overall score to an individual data element that can be audited. That auditable transparency is a powerful addition for credibility and customer satisfaction.
