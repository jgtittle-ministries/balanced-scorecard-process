# How This Book Came to Be

The Balanced Scorecard Process was not invented in a single moment. It grew, over many years of consulting work, out of a pattern that kept showing up across very different client engagements: a contract would be in place; the contractually-defined Service Level Agreements would be green; and the customer would still be unhappy. Sometimes deeply unhappy. Often unhappy in ways the existing measurement structure could not see.

What was missing was a way to surface the competing values the customer was actually paying for, agree on how much weight each one carried, and build a measurement structure that reflected those weights in a form the line managers could act on week to week. The standard industry SLAs measured useful things, but they did not measure *enough* things, and they did not measure them in proportions that matched what the customer actually cared about.

I started building the methodology in the early 2000s. I borrowed deliberately from existing work — Kaplan and Norton's original Balanced Scorecard, the Saaty Analytic Hierarchy Process and the Expert Choice tool that implements it, the SEI's metrics template and the Goal-Question-Metric (GQM) process, statistical process control for setting target ranges, and the Catalyst workshop methodology I learned in consulting work. The contribution of BSCP is not in any single one of those tools. It is in the **assembly** — the particular way they are combined into a process that, run in sequence with the right participants, consistently produces an operationally useful, behavior-aligned, customer-owned scorecard.

The first draft of this book was written in 2002. The draft has been revised across many years, with new examples and case studies added as the methodology was applied in new engagements. The 2015 sanitized version of the metric-options appendix represented a substantial reworking. The current public publication is the latest pass.

## Why publish it now, and why publish it this way

For a long time I wanted to publish this book but did not have the publishing path I wanted. Conventional book publishing felt slow and inflexible for a work that has continued to evolve with each engagement; self-publishing in a static form felt like it would lose the iterative-improvement character that made the methodology effective in the first place.

The format you are reading — a Markdown-based static site, version-controlled in a public GitHub repository, with a small pencil icon on every page that lets any reader propose an edit — fits the spirit of the methodology. The methodology is itself iterative: every 12-to-18 months a working BSCP gets reviewed and refreshed, with about a third of the metrics replaced based on what was learned in the prior period. A book on that methodology should be similarly revisable.

So the book is being published progressively, in public. Readers who find errors, gaps, or opportunities for clarification are invited to suggest changes directly. See [Contributing](contributing.md) for how.

## Acknowledgments

The methodology was shaped by many client teams, many delivery teams, and many candid conversations over years. I owe debts to all of them. Specific acknowledgments will be added as the book matures, with the consent of the named individuals.

I owe a particular debt to the unnamed senior leader whose words ground Chapter 1 — the one who framed the challenge as "a holistic and balanced approach to measurement" embedded in "a formal governance mechanism that lives and breathes with the reality of changes in the client and business situations." That framing has been my own working description of what BSCP is for ever since.
