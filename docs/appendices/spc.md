**Background for Statistical Process Control (SPC)**

- Before industrialization, individuals controlled quality. Some still do in small businesses, but many do not
- After industrialization, individuals had less control for individual quality. Example – Automobile production
- Came from the manufacturing shop floor
- In the 1920s, Walter Shewhart (Bell Telephone), In the 1950s W. Edward Demings contributed significantly
- WWII really spurred the development of SPC


## Slide 3: 3

![Slide 3](spc/slide-003.png)

**Background (Additional)**

- Before SPC, quality was (is) normally checked at the end of the production of the product (could be many “products” in the production of an automobile)
- After SPC, quality can be checked during the manufacturing process and not only at the end of production, though this is not always done.
- SPC allowed us to conceptually move from checking EACH product to a SAMPLE of products – much less costly


## Slide 4: 4

![Slide 4](spc/slide-004.png)

**Definition**

- Statistical Process Control (SPC)
- can be defined as a collection of strategies, techniques, and actions taken by an organization to ensure that they are producing a quality product or providing a quality service.
- Key word for SPC is variance or standard deviation


## Slide 5: 5

![Slide 5](spc/slide-005.png)

**Variation**

- There are many reasons why production processes demonstrate variability.
- Some of these reasons include
- equipment wear-out or failure
- unclear work standards
- non-homogenous raw materials
- environmental factors, such as temperature


## Slide 6: 6

![Slide 6](spc/slide-006.png)

**Variation (Additional)**

- Without SPC
- With 100% inspection, all items that are manufactured are checked to see if they meet target values.
- This method can be advantageous when questions of health, safety, or liability are at issue.
- However, this method may be costly.
- It can also be difficult to correct errors at the end of production rather than improving production in order to eliminate errors.
- With SPC
- Sampling inspection involves inspecting a sample of items.
- On the basis of these data, this process is declared to be "in control" or "not
- Sampling inspection is a more cost-effective way than 100% inspection to examine the "health" of the production process.
- Because it is cost-effective, it can be used at several points in the process in order to identify problems early.


## Slide 7: 7

![Slide 7](spc/slide-007.png)

**Control Charts 1**

- Control charts were first developed by Walter Shewhart in the 1920s to control in-process manufacturing operations.
- A control chart can now be defined as a time-series plot of a statistic - such as a sample mean, standard deviation, proportion, or range - with a centerline and upper and lower control limits.
- The upper and lower control limits (UCL and LCL, respectively) demonstrate how much variation from the target value can be expected for a given sample size.
- When the bounds of these limits are breached, a process is deemed out of control.
- A quality control engineer sets the upper and lower control limits so that there will be little probability of exceeding them when the process is in control.
- The formulas for the UCL and LCL depend upon the variability of the process and the sample size. They also depend upon the quantity being monitored, such as the mean, the standard deviation, or the proportion of defective


## Slide 8: 8

![Slide 8](spc/slide-008.png)

**Assignable vs. Chance Variation**

- Control charts ultimately identify when assignable causes of variation enter the process.
- Assignable variation is not random and can usually be eliminated or reduced by investigating the problem and finding the causes.
- Chance variation, on the other hand, is random in nature and cannot be eliminated unless there is a major change in the equipment or material used in the process
- Internal machine friction, variations in material or process conditions, atmospheric conditions, and vibrations transmitted to a machine from, say, a passing forklift are a few examples of sources of chance variation.
- In certain circumstances, some of these sources of variability can be controlled.
- If a hole drilled into a sheet of steel is too large because of a dull drill, the drill may be sharpened or a new drill inserted.
- This is an example of assignable variation.


## Slide 9: 9

![Slide 9](spc/slide-009.png)

**Control Charts 2**

- If a production process is in control, the sample mean has over a 99% chance of falling in this interval.
- A sample mean outside this interval may indicate problems with the process.
- If a sample mean falls above the UCL or below the LCL, the production department must stop the process and try to fix the problem.
- The pattern in a control chart may indicate the type of problem that has occurred.
- For example, a large sudden jump in the mean might indicate that a part has broken.
