## Slide 3: Analytical Hierarchy Process

![Slide 3](ahp/slide-003.png)

A structured decision-making process, based on Multi Attribute Utility Theory… developed by Dr Thomas L Saaty in the early 1970sProvides a basis for collaborating to reach decision consensusModels the problem as a hierarchy of criteria that the decision needs to take into accountApplies ratio scales in judging, level by level, the relative importance of the criteria, compared with each otherHandles tangible and intangible criteriaBasic stagesConstruct hierarchy of homogeneous clustered criteriaEstablish relative levels of importance/preference, by levelEnsure logical consistency, iterating where necessary

## Slide 4: Collaboration to reach consensus

![Slide 4](ahp/slide-004.png)

Framework for group participationStakeholder groups score their criteria comparisons independently Can use modal values, geometric means, and Delphi approach,  but better, through debate and compromise, to …Expose the differences and what accounts for themCapture the informationArrive at consensus as to what scores should be attached to the criteria (as well as relative performance of options) When applied in BSCP, only the client would score the criteria comparisons

## Slide 6: Expressing judgements

![Slide 6](ahp/slide-006.png)

Ratio scales Avoids problems with mixed measurement scalesMakes a virtue of stakeholder judgementUsually a pair-wise comparison of criteria within each level of the hierarchy… based on relative importance/priority to the overall decisionIn addition, when options are being evaluated, each option is pair-wise compared with the others… as to their performance against the criteria

## Slide 41: Multi-Attribute Utility Theory (MAUT)

![Slide 41](ahp/slide-041.png)

A structured approach to decision makingGiven a set of objectives, derive measurable criteria and use them to evaluate the relative preferences (utilities) of a number of optionsRelies heavily on stakeholder judgementGenerally:Can be viewed as a ‘solution’, and a ‘model’Solution, i.e. it provides an answerModel, i.e. it supports sensitivity analysis

## Slide 44: Steps in multi-attribute utility analysis

![Slide 44](ahp/slide-044.png)

Define the overall context and objectivesDefine the options and relevant criteria Evaluate each option on each criterion and remove ‘dominated’ optionsAssign relative weights (utilities) to the criteria (see next slide) Combine the criteria weights and evaluations to yield an overall evaluation of each option Perform sensitivity analysis and make a decision
